The Data Centre Gap

Africa's total data centre capacity is estimated at less than 200MW â€” roughly equivalent to a single large hyperscale campus in Virginia or Northern Virginia. The entire continent has fewer data centres than the city of London.

This capacity is concentrated in just three countries: South Africa (the dominant hub, with ~60% of total capacity), Nigeria, and Kenya. Most African countries have effectively zero commercial data centre capacity.

The capacity math

Training a single frontier-class LLM can consume gigawatt-months of compute. A single training run for GPT-5.6 Sol or Claude Opus 4.8 likely required more electricity than all of Africa's data centres combined consume in a year. The inference gap is smaller but still vast â€” serving millions of users requires distributed, low-latency infrastructure that simply doesn't exist at scale on the continent.

Connectivity & Energy

Data centres are useless without connectivity and power. Submarine cable capacity to Africa has grown significantly â€” projects like 2Africa, Equiano, and SEA-ME-WE 5 have added substantial bandwidth â€” but last-mile connectivity remains expensive and unreliable.

Energy is the harder constraint. Data centres need reliable, 24/7 power. In Nigeria, the national grid experiences multiple collapses per year; in South Africa, load-shedding (planned blackouts) has been a recurring problem. Off-grid solutions (solar + battery + diesel) add cost that makes African data centres more expensive to operate than their European or US counterparts. This creates a vicious cycle: higher costs mean less investment, which means fewer facilities, which means higher costs for those that do exist.

The energy cost penalty

A 2025 study by the African Data Centres Association found that the total cost of ownership for a medium-sized data centre in Lagos is approximately 30â€“40% higher than an equivalent facility in Frankfurt or Virginia, driven primarily by energy costs and backup generation requirements.

National AI Strategies

Only 7 of 54 African nations have published national AI strategies: South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya, Rwanda, Mauritius, Tunisia, and Ghana. Most are aspirational rather than fully funded. Few include specific provisions for compute infrastructure, data centre development, or energy planning for AI workloads.

The African Union's Continental AI Strategy (draft, 2025) identifies infrastructure as a priority but has no binding mechanism for member states to act on it.
